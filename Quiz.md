## 3. Quick Review

---

### **Key Formulas and Concepts**

- **Order of Operations:** Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction (PEMDAS/BODMAS)
- **Rounding:** Look at the digit to the right of the place you are rounding to.
- **Even/Odd Properties:**  
  - Even + Even = Even  
  - Odd + Odd = Even  
  - Even + Odd = Odd  
  - Odd × Odd = Odd  
  - Even × Even = Even  
  - Even × Odd = Even
- **Mathematical Notation:**  
  - Product: multiplication (\( ab \) or \( a \times b \))
  - Sum: addition (\( a + b \))
  - Difference: subtraction (\( a - b \))
  - Quotient: division (\( a \div b \) or \( \frac{a}{b} \))
- **Logical Reasoning:**  
  - Contrapositive of "If A, then B" is "If not B, then not A" (logically equivalent).

---

### **Common Mistake Warnings**

- **Skipping order of operations:** Always follow PEMDAS/BODMAS.
- **Rounding errors:** Double-check which digit to round and whether to round up or down.
- **Misreading notation:** "Product" means multiply, "sum" means add.
- **Assuming properties:** Not all combinations of even/odd numbers behave the same way.

---

### **Strategy Reminders**

- **Estimate first:** For large calculations, estimation helps check if your answer is reasonable.
- **Substitute values carefully:** Plug in values step by step.
- **Check for logical equivalence:** Know the contrapositive for logic questions.
- **Work systematically:** For counting problems, list possibilities in order.

### **Problem 1 (Basic)**

**Problem:** What is the greatest common divisor (GCD) of 18 and 24?  
**Options:** A) 2 B) 3 C) 6 D) 12 E) 24  

**Solution:**  
- List the factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18  
- List the factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24  
- Common factors: 1, 2, 3, 6  
- Greatest is **6**.

**Answer:** **C) 6**

**Key Concept:**  
GCD is the largest number dividing both numbers.

**Tip:**  
Prime factorization method:  
- 18 = 2 × 3²  
- 24 = 2³ × 3  
- GCD: 2¹ × 3¹ = 6

### **Problem 15 (Advanced)**

**Problem:** A three-digit number has a remainder of 1 when divided by 7, a remainder of 2 when divided by 8, and a remainder of 3 when divided by 9. What is the smallest such number?  
**Options:** A) 251 B) 337 C) 521 D) 577 E) 601  

**Solution:**  
Let \( n \) be the number.  
We have:  
\( n \equiv 1 \pmod{7} \)  
\( n \equiv 2 \pmod{8} \)  
\( n \equiv 3 \pmod{9} \)

Let’s look for \( n \) by checking the options:

- **A) 251**  
251 ÷ 7 = 35, remainder 6 (not 1)  
- **B) 337**  
337 ÷ 7 = 48, remainder 1 ✓  
337 ÷ 8 = 42, remainder 1 (not 2)  
- **C) 521**  
521 ÷ 7 = 74, remainder 3  
- **D) 577**  
577 ÷ 7 = 82, remainder 3  
- **E) 601**  
601 ÷ 7 = 85, remainder 6  

Try to solve algebraically:

Let’s find \( n \) such that \( n \equiv 1 \pmod{7} \) and \( n \equiv 2 \pmod{8} \).

Let \( n = 7a + 1 \).  
Set \( 7a + 1 \equiv 2 \pmod{8} \implies 7a \equiv 1 \pmod{8} \).  
7a ≡ 1 mod 8  
Try a = 7: 7×7=49 ≡ 1 mod 8 (since 49-48=1)  
So, a = 7, 15, 23, ... (a ≡ 7 mod 8)

So, \( a = 8b + 7 \), so  
\( n = 7a + 1 = 7(8b+7) + 1 = 56b + 49 + 1 = 56b + 50 \)

Now, \( n \equiv 3 \pmod{9} \implies 56b + 50 \equiv 3 \pmod{9} \)  
56 mod 9 = 2 (since 9×6=54, 56-54=2)  
So, \( 2b + 50 \equiv 3 \pmod{9} \)  
50 mod 9 = 5 (since 9×5=45, 50-45=5)  
So, \( 2b + 5 \equiv 3 \pmod{9} \implies 2b \equiv -2 \pmod{9} \implies 2b \equiv 7 \pmod{9} \)

Now, 2b ≡ 7 mod 9  
Try b = 8: 2×8=16 ≡ 16-9=7 mod 9  
So, b = 8

Now, \( n = 56b + 50 = 56×8 + 50 = 448 + 50 = 498 \)

Is 498 three digits? Yes.

Check:  
498 ÷ 7 = 71 remainder 1 ✓  
498 ÷ 8 = 62 remainder 2 ✓  
498 ÷ 9 = 55 remainder 3 ✓

So, the smallest such number is **498**. But 498 is not in the options. Let's check for the next possible value.

The next b satisfying 2b ≡ 7 mod 9 is b = 8 + 9 = 17

n = 56×17 + 50 = 952 + 50 = 1002 (not three-digit)

So, 498 is the smallest. Since it's not in the options, perhaps there's a typo in the options. Let's check the closest one: 577.

577 ÷ 7 = 82 remainder 3  
So, not matching.

Alternatively, check 601:

601 ÷ 7 = 85 remainder 6  
601 ÷ 8 = 75 remainder 1  
601 ÷ 9 = 66 remainder 7  

None of the options fit. The correct answer is **498**, but since it's not listed, this is a good opportunity to remind students to check their work and understand the process.

**Answer:** **498** (not in options; check process and options in real AMC 8)

## 3. Quick Review

---

### **Key Formulas & Concepts**

- **Factors:** Numbers that divide evenly into another number.
- **Multiples:** Results of multiplying a number by integers.
- **Divisibility:** Rules to check if a number divides another (e.g., ends in 0 for 10, sum of digits for 3, etc.).
- **Prime Numbers:** Numbers greater than 1 with only two divisors: 1 and itself.
- **Prime Factorization:** Writing a number as a product of primes.
- **GCD (Greatest Common Divisor):** Largest number dividing both numbers.
    - Use prime factorization: take lowest powers.
- **LCM (Least Common Multiple):** Smallest number divisible by both.
    - Use prime factorization: take highest powers.
- **Remainders:** What's left after division.
- **Modular Arithmetic:** \( a \equiv r \pmod{n} \) means \( a \) leaves remainder \( r \) when divided by \( n \).

---

### **Common Mistake Warnings**

- **Confusing GCD and LCM:** Remember, GCD is for dividing, LCM is for multiplying.
- **Forgetting to include 1 and the number itself as factors.**
- **Mixing up remainder and quotient.**
- **Not checking all options for prime numbers.**
- **Missing numbers in a list (like all primes less than a certain number).**

---

### **Strategy Reminders**

- **List factors or multiples systematically.**
- **Use prime factorization for GCD/LCM questions.**
- **Check divisibility with rules (e.g., sum of digits for 3, last digit for 2, 5, 10).**
- **For remainders, use modular arithmetic or repeated subtraction.**
- **For advanced modular problems, use the Chinese Remainder Theorem or systematic checking.**
- **Double-check calculations, especially for multi-step problems.**

## 3. Quick Review

### Key Formulas and Concepts

- **Integer Properties:**  
  - Addition/Subtraction: Same sign, add; different sign, subtract and keep sign of larger.
  - Product of two negatives is positive.
  - Consecutive integers: \( x, x+1, x+2, \ldots \)

- **Fractions:**  
  - Addition/Subtraction: Use common denominators.
  - Multiplication: Multiply numerators and denominators.
  - Division: Multiply by reciprocal.

- **Decimals and Percentages:**  
  - Decimal to percent: Move decimal two places right (\( \times 100 \)).
  - Percent to decimal: Move decimal two places left (\( \div 100 \)).
  - To find \( x\% \) of \( y \): \( x\% \times y = \frac{x}{100} \times y \).

- **Comparing and Ordering:**  
  - Convert all numbers to decimals (or fractions) for easy comparison.
  - \( n\% = \frac{n}{100} \).

### Common Mistake Warnings

- Forgetting to use common denominators when adding/subtracting fractions.
- Not converting percentages to decimals before multiplying.
- Mixing up the direction of inequalities when comparing negatives.
- Forgetting to simplify fractions at the end.

### Strategy Reminders

- For word problems, define variables and set up equations.
- For repeating decimals, use algebraic manipulation.
- Always check if you can simplify your final answer.
- Double-check units and forms (fraction, decimal, percent) before comparing or ordering.

**Problem:** A number is increased by 20% and then decreased by 20%. What is the net percent change?

**Solution:**  
Let the original number be 100.  
Increase by 20%: 100 → 120  
Decrease by 20%: 120 × 0.8 = 96  
Change: 100 → 96, so decrease of 4.

Net percent change = \( \frac{4}{100} \times 100 = 4\% \) decrease.

**Answer:** **C) 4% decrease**

**Tip:** Successive percent changes are not additive.

#### **15. (Advanced) Solving Proportions**

**Problem:** If \( x : y = 2 : 5 \) and \( y : z = 4 : 3 \), what is \( x : z \)?

**Solution:**  
First, make y the same in both ratios.  
\( x : y = 2 : 5 \)  
\( y : z = 4 : 3 \)

Find LCM of y values (5 and 4) = 20.

Rewrite:  
\( x : y = 8 : 20 \) (multiply both by 4)  
\( y : z = 20 : 15 \) (multiply both by 5)

So, \( x : y : z = 8 : 20 : 15 \)

So, \( x : z = 8 : 15 \)

**Answer:** **A) 8:15**

**Tip:** Express all ratios with the same middle term.

- **Proportion:** An equation stating two ratios are equal, \( \frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d} \).
- **Percent:** A ratio out of 100, \( x\% = \frac{x}{100} \).
- **Solving Proportions:** Cross-multiply to solve, \( a \times d = b \times c \).
- **Fraction to Percent:** Multiply by 100.
- **Percent Change:** \( \frac{\text{new} - \text{original}}{\text{original}} \times 100\% \).

- **Successive Percents:** Increasing and then decreasing by the same percent does *not* return you to the original value.
- **Total Parts in Ratios:** Always sum all parts before dividing the total.

## **3. Quick Review**

---

### **Key Formulas and Concepts**

- **Sum of interior angles of polygon:** (n – 2) × 180°, where n = number of sides.
- **Interior angle of regular polygon:** [(n – 2) × 180°]/n
- **Perimeter of rectangle:** 2 × (length + width)
- **Area of rectangle:** length × width
- **Area of triangle:** ½ × base × height
- **Area of right triangle:** ½ × (leg₁) × (leg₂)
- **Area of square:** side²
- **Circumference of circle:** 2πr
- **Area of circle:** πr²
- **Diameter of circle:** 2r
- **Pythagorean theorem:** a² + b² = c² (for right triangles)
- **Chord length in circle:** 2r × sin(θ/2), where θ is the central angle in radians

---

### **Common Mistake Warnings**

- Don’t confuse perimeter with area.
- Always use the correct formula for each shape.
- Double-check units and whether you’re being asked for length, area, or angle.
- For polygons, make sure you use the correct value of n (number of sides).
- For triangles, always check if it’s a right triangle before using Pythagoras or right triangle area formula.

---

### **Strategy Reminders**

- Draw a diagram for geometry problems.
- Label all known values.
- For polygons, memorize the angle sum and regular angle formulas.
- For circles, remember relationships between radius, diameter, circumference, and area.
- For advanced problems, combine formulas (e.g., use both area and Pythagoras for rectangles with diagonals).
- Check your answer choices against calculations, especially for tricky advanced problems.

  - A 2D shape that can be folded into a 3D solid.

- **Cross-Section:**  
  - The shape you get when you cut straight through a solid.

## 3. Quick Review

---

### **Key Formulas and Concepts**

- **Counting Principle:** If there are _m_ ways to do one thing, and _n_ ways to do another, there are _m × n_ ways to do both.
- **Permutations (order matters):**  
  - _n!_ ways to arrange _n_ objects.  
  - Number of ways to arrange _k_ out of _n_ objects: P(n, k) = n! / (n–k)!
- **Combinations (order doesn’t matter):**  
  - Number of ways to choose _k_ out of _n_: C(n, k) = n! / [k!(n–k)!]
- **Subsets:** A set with _n_ elements has 2^n subsets.

### **Common Mistake Warnings**

- **Don’t mix up permutations and combinations!** Use permutations when order matters, combinations when it doesn’t.
- **Don’t forget to subtract for restrictions** (e.g., "no repeats", "must be even", "no two together").
- **Careful with digits:** If leading zeros are not allowed, adjust accordingly.

### **Strategy Reminders**

- **Draw diagrams or list small cases** to avoid overcounting or missing cases.
- **Break complex problems into steps** (e.g., choose, then arrange).
- **Check for restrictions** in the problem statement.
- **Use the multiplication principle** for independent choices.
- **When in doubt, try small numbers** to see the pattern.
